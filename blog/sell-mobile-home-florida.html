Title vs Deed - The Critical Distinction

The single most important factor in selling a mobile home in Florida is whether it is classified as personal property or real property. This distinction affects how you sell, how the buyer finances, and how the transfer is documented.

Personal property (title): If your mobile home sits on land you do not own (such as in a mobile home park or on rented land), it is classified as personal property. It has a vehicle title issued by the Florida Department of Highway Safety and Motor Vehicles (DHSMV), similar to a car. The sale is documented by title transfer, not a deed.

Real property (deed): If your mobile home sits on land you own and the title has been "retired" (surrendered to the county property appraiser), the home and land together are classified as real property. The sale is documented by a deed, just like any other house. The home appears on the property tax rolls as an improvement to the land.

How to check: If you pay separate lot rent to a park or landowner, your mobile home is almost certainly personal property. If you own the land and your property tax bill shows the mobile home as part of the real estate assessment, it is likely real property. You can verify with the county property appraiser's office or check if a vehicle title exists through the DHSMV.

This distinction matters enormously because most mortgage lenders will not finance personal property mobile homes. Only real property mobile homes qualify for conventional mortgages, FHA, and VA loans.

DMV Title Transfer Process

If your mobile home is personal property (has a vehicle title), here is the transfer process:

  1. Obtain the title. If you have a loan on the mobile home, contact your lender for a payoff amount and lien release. You cannot transfer the title with an active lien.
  2. Complete the title transfer form. Both seller and buyer sign the back of the title certificate or complete HSMV Form 82040.
  3. Pay applicable taxes and fees. The buyer pays 6% Florida sales tax on the purchase price (or the NADA book value, whichever is higher). Registration and title fees are approximately $75-$100.
  4. Submit to the county tax collector. The buyer takes the signed title, bill of sale, and payment to any Florida county tax collector's office to complete the transfer.

Important: the 6% sales tax on personal property mobile homes can be a surprise to both buyers and sellers. On a $50,000 mobile home, that is $3,000 in sales tax the buyer must pay on top of the purchase price. This reduces what buyers can afford to pay for the home itself.

Selling a Mobile Home With Land

If you own both the mobile home and the land, your situation is more straightforward and your property is typically worth more:

  • Retire the title first. If you have not already, you should retire the mobile home's vehicle title by filing an Application to Retire Mobile Home Title (HSMV Form 85133) with the county property appraiser. This converts it from personal property to real property, making it eligible for conventional financing.
  • Sell like any other home. Once the title is retired, you sell through a standard real estate transaction with a deed, title insurance, and closing agent. The buyer can use a mortgage, FHA loan, VA loan, or cash.
  • Appraisal considerations. Mobile homes on owned land appraise differently than site-built homes. Age, condition, whether it was built after 1976 (HUD code), whether it has a permanent foundation, and whether the title has been retired all affect value.

Properties with a mobile home on owned land - especially on acreage - are attractive to cash buyers and investors. The land provides underlying value regardless of the mobile home's condition.

Park Approval and Lot Rent Considerations

If your mobile home is in a park, additional factors come into play:

Park approval of buyer. Most mobile home parks require approval of any new buyer/resident. This can include background checks, income verification, and age requirements (many parks are 55+). If the park rejects the buyer, the sale cannot proceed.

Lot rent transfer. The buyer will need to establish a lot rental agreement with the park. Current lot rent amounts directly affect what a buyer can pay for the home - high lot rent ($800-$1,200/month) reduces the home's effective value because buyers compare total housing cost against alternatives.

Park right of first refusal. Some parks have the right to match any offer on a mobile home in their community. Check your lot lease for this provision.

Park rules on home age and condition. Some parks will not allow the sale of homes older than a certain age or homes that do not meet specific condition standards. If the park requires repairs or upgrades before approving a transfer, those costs fall on the seller.

Florida Statute 723. The Florida Mobile Home Act (Chapter 723) provides protections for mobile home owners in parks, including provisions about lot rental increases, eviction, and home sale rights. Familiarize yourself with your rights before selling.

Lender Restrictions on Mobile Homes

Financing a mobile home is significantly harder than financing a site-built home, which directly impacts your pool of potential buyers:

  • Personal property mobile homes: No conventional mortgage, FHA, or VA financing available. Buyers must use cash, chattel loans (personal property loans with higher rates - typically 8-12%), or seller financing.
  • Real property mobile homes (title retired, on owned land): Eligible for FHA (if built after 1976 and on permanent foundation), VA, and some conventional loans. However, many lenders have additional requirements: minimum square footage (typically 400+ sqft), double-wide or larger, permanent foundation certification, and age limits (some lenders will not finance homes older than 20-30 years).
  • Manufactured vs modular: Modular homes (built to state building code and assembled on-site) are financed like site-built homes. Manufactured homes (built to federal HUD code in a factory) face the restrictions above.

The financing restrictions mean that traditional MLS listing often produces a small, limited buyer pool for mobile homes - especially personal property units in parks. This is the primary reason mobile homes take longer to sell on the MLS and often sell below comparable site-built homes.

The Cash Buyer Advantage for Mobile Homes

Cash buyers are particularly well-suited for mobile home transactions because they eliminate every financing hurdle:

  • No lender approval needed - no appraisal, no foundation certification, no age restrictions
  • Can purchase personal property mobile homes (in parks) without chattel loan complications
  • No park lender-approval delays
  • Can close in 7-14 days once the title or deed is clear
  • Purchase as-is - no repair requirements

If you are selling a mobile home in Florida - whether in a park, on rented land, or on your own property - a cash offer gives you a fast, certain path to closing without the complications that make mobile home sales uniquely challenging.

FAQ

If the mobile home is personal property, you need the title to transfer ownership. If the title is lost, you can apply for a duplicate title through the Florida DHSMV. If the title has been retired (converted to real property), you sell by deed instead.

The buyer pays 6% Florida sales tax on personal property mobile homes (title transfer). If the mobile home is real property (sold by deed with the land), no sales tax applies - only documentary stamp tax like any other real estate transaction.

On the MLS, mobile homes typically take 60-120 days to sell due to limited buyer financing options. Cash sales can close in 7-14 days. Park homes may take longer if park approval is required.
