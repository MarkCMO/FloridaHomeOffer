Types of Code Violations

Florida properties can accumulate a wide range of code violations, and understanding the type of violation you are dealing with affects both your options and the likely sale price. Code violations fall into several major categories, each with different severity levels and resolution paths.

Property maintenance violations are the most common. These include overgrown vegetation, exterior paint deterioration, broken windows, damaged roofing, non-functional gutters, and accumulated debris. Florida municipalities enforce these through property maintenance codes that require homes to meet minimum exterior standards. While individually minor, these violations can accrue daily fines that add up quickly when left unaddressed for months or years.

Building code violations involve structural, electrical, plumbing, or mechanical work done without permits or not to code. Unpermitted additions are extremely common in Florida - enclosed patios, added bedrooms, converted garages, and backyard structures built without permits. When these unpermitted modifications are discovered during a sale, they create significant complications. The new Florida Building Code requires all work to be permitted and inspected, and retroactive permitting often requires bringing the entire addition up to current code standards, which can cost thousands more than the original work.

Health and safety violations are the most serious category. These include non-functional smoke detectors, blocked egress, exposed wiring, sewage issues, and conditions that present immediate danger to occupants. These violations often result from deferred maintenance, hoarding conditions, or failed DIY repairs. Health and safety violations typically carry the highest fines and the most aggressive enforcement timelines.

Zoning violations involve using the property in ways not permitted by its zoning classification. Common examples in Florida include operating a business from a residentially zoned property, maintaining more dwelling units than permitted (such as an illegal apartment conversion), and exceeding setback or lot coverage limits with additions or structures. Zoning violations can require demolition of non-conforming structures in some cases.

Fines and How They Accumulate

Code violation fines in Florida vary by municipality but follow a general pattern. After a violation is cited, you typically receive a notice with a compliance deadline - usually 14-30 days for most violations, or as little as 24-48 hours for imminent safety hazards. If you fail to comply by the deadline, daily fines begin.

Initial fine amounts range from $50 to $250 per day per violation in most Florida jurisdictions. Repeat violations or continued non-compliance can see fines escalate to $500 per day. Some municipalities impose maximum caps on fine accumulation, while others allow unlimited accrual. A single $100/day violation left unaddressed for one year generates $36,500 in fines. Multiple concurrent violations can push annual fine accumulation to $50,000-$100,000 or more.

The Special Magistrate or Code Enforcement Board in your jurisdiction has the authority to set and adjust fines. They also have the authority to reduce or waive accumulated fines when a violation is corrected or the property is sold to a buyer who commits to remediation. This reduction power is critical for selling properties with large accumulated fines, as full payment of accrued fines would make many sales financially impossible.

What many homeowners do not realize is that code violation fines accrue automatically once imposed - you do not receive daily notices. The fine clock starts at the compliance deadline and runs continuously until the violation is corrected and the property passes re-inspection. Many sellers discover tens of thousands in accumulated fines only when they attempt to sell and a title search reveals the liens.

Lien Risk and Title Issues

Accumulated code violation fines become liens against the property when the municipality records them with the county clerk. In Florida, code enforcement liens are considered government liens and have priority similar to tax liens - they must be satisfied before a property can transfer clear title. This means code violation liens cannot simply be ignored; they must be addressed at or before closing.

Code enforcement liens survive foreclosure in Florida under certain circumstances. Florida Statute 162.09(3) provides that code enforcement liens recorded prior to other encumbrances may maintain priority even through a foreclosure sale. This makes code violation liens particularly troublesome because they can affect the property regardless of ownership changes.

Title companies will flag code enforcement liens during the title search, and most will not issue title insurance with unresolved liens. This effectively blocks any traditional sale until the liens are cleared. The process of clearing code enforcement liens involves either paying the full amount, negotiating a reduction with the municipality, or demonstrating that the violations have been corrected and requesting lien release through the code enforcement board.

For sellers facing significant code enforcement liens, the negotiation process can take 30-90 days. Cash buyers experienced with code violation properties often handle this negotiation themselves, working directly with the municipality to reduce fines and clear liens as part of the closing process. This saves the seller from navigating the bureaucratic process while still enabling the sale to proceed.

Resolution at Closing

When selling a property with code violations to a cash buyer, resolution typically happens in one of three ways. The most common approach is a negotiated lien reduction where the buyer (or their attorney) works with the municipality to reduce accumulated fines in exchange for a commitment to bring the property into compliance. Florida municipalities generally prefer this approach because they get a responsible new owner who will fix the violations, which is better than an indefinitely non-compliant property.

The second approach is paying fines from sale proceeds at closing. The title company withholds the lien amounts from the seller's proceeds and pays the municipality directly. This is straightforward but can significantly reduce the seller's net proceeds, especially when fines have accumulated over several years.

The third approach is an escrow holdback, where the buyer deposits funds in escrow to cover violation remediation costs. The municipality agrees to release or reduce the lien upon completion of repairs, and the escrow funds are released to the contractor or back to the buyer as repairs are verified. This approach requires a cooperative municipality and an experienced title company, but it allows the sale to close before all violations are physically corrected.

County vs City Enforcement

Understanding whether your violations come from the county or a city municipality matters for resolution strategy. In Florida, unincorporated areas are enforced by the county code enforcement department, while incorporated cities enforce their own codes within city limits. Some properties fall under both jurisdictions for different types of violations.

County enforcement tends to be more uniform across a jurisdiction, with standardized fine schedules and resolution processes. Larger counties like Miami-Dade, Broward, Hillsborough, and Duval have established code enforcement boards with regular hearing schedules and formalized lien reduction programs. County-level violations are often easier to negotiate because the process is well-documented and predictable.

City enforcement varies dramatically between municipalities. Some smaller Florida cities have aggressive enforcement with steep fines, while others are more lenient. Cities like Miami, Tampa, Jacksonville, and Orlando have dedicated code enforcement departments with significant staff and resources. Smaller municipalities may have a single code enforcement officer covering all violations, which can make scheduling hearings and negotiations more challenging but also means there is more flexibility in resolution approaches.

Regardless of the enforcement jurisdiction, the key to resolving code violations during a sale is engaging early, demonstrating good faith, and working with a buyer or attorney experienced in the local code enforcement process. Cash buyers who regularly purchase properties with code violations maintain relationships with local code enforcement offices throughout Florida, which significantly streamlines the resolution process.
